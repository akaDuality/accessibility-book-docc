# What is accessibility?

Accessibility of an application is the adaptation of its interface for different groups of people.

## Definition

If a person sees poorly (or has lost their sight completely), can't hear, can't move, and yet still uses the application fully, then it is accessible.

- **Subtitles**: don't know English but want to watch a new series on Netflix? There are subtitles and a full translation into Russian.
- **Edge swipe**: can't shift the phone into one hand to press the back button because you're holding a child in the other? You can swipe from the edge of the screen.
- **Dark theme**: reading a book before bed? Dark theme won't blind you.

This is a kind of "standard" accessibility for everyone. You could probably live without these features, but with them it's more convenient. If you take each of these examples and push it further, it starts to help people with health conditions, for whom these "features" become essential.
- Older people can use a phone without glasses by increasing the font size or turning on screen zoom.
- Audio description instead of subtitles will tell a blind person what's happening on screen, and a special VoiceOver gesture helps them go back to the previous screen.
- Dark theme helps people with photophobia.
- Vibration on a watch helps people who can't hear know about an incoming call.

And for those who can't move at all, a single twitch of the cheek lets them interact with the world: giving the phone commands like "select" and "next item." Perhaps this will help someone write a book about how the universe works.

I'll talk about the most severe impairments, where at first it isn't even clear how a person could use a touchscreen phone at all. It might seem that a phone with buttons would be more convenient for a blind person, but in fact the world changed after the release of the iPhone 3GS. You can read more about this in the article [«Accessibility on iOS started with 36 seconds.»](https://habr.com/en/companies/dododev/articles/559622/)

## Situational accessibility

![accessibility-types](accessibility-types)

Accessibility can be permanent or temporary:
- Permanent: a person can't see, can't hear, can't hold a phone, can't focus on a task
- Temporary: a driver is focused on the road, a mom is holding a child, the phone is in your pocket while running, you broke an arm, and so on.

If you look at product scenarios through this lens, it may turn out that, for example, Siri support helps both groups of people. Or if you want people to drop by your pizzeria more often in the morning for coffee, maybe you should stop building them on the second floor, so all the moms with strollers can stop in on a walk?

Or take "accessibility isn't needed in interfaces for employees." And then it turns out that warehouse inventory apps are run on a cheap Android wrapped in a case, with a cracked screen, on minimum brightness to save battery.
