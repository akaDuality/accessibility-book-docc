# Why do accessibility?

Accessibility is useful for developers, important for companies, and is a skill that can be learned.

## Accessibility is a property of a good interface

As a mobile developer, I build interfaces for people: convenient, beautiful, and simple. There's a lot of knowledge in this work: the laws of interaction with interfaces, perception of information, and influence on emotions. At the same time, the whole industry tries to reach as many users as possible — many apps work for the whole country or the whole world.

**What properties does a good interface have?** It is clear, responsive, beautiful, fast, and... accessible. Because if you can't use the service, *then you can't use the service!*

Can an inaccessible interface be called high quality? Only in rare cases. For example, if your product is used by a narrow group of people and you're absolutely sure they're as prepared as astronauts.

For popular applications, the picture is different: out of millions of users, very many people have impairments. Sometimes the limitations are small, sometimes serious, but they all change a way of life. In the past, such conditions could create insurmountable barriers in a person's life, but today life can be comfortable, full, and interesting, thanks to the technology everyone carries in their pocket.

## The iPhone has already done a lot for you

The coolest thing is that the iPhone already contains all the necessary tools: a speech synthesizer, a multitouch display, a head tracker, and several iOS modules that provide accessibility. You just need to help them a little: tell these technologies how to interact with your interface.

## Why is this useful for developers?

Adapting an application for blind users helps you understand what an "interface" is. It shows the complexity of the world and the paths of interaction: people don't just tap the screen, they can also control with a keyboard, voice, or gestures. A high-quality interface provides all of this.

By turning a graphical interface into an audio one, you better understand how our perception works. We quickly read a mental model from the screen, and then it's our understanding of that model that does the work. Someone who can work with information at this level can spot problems in graphical design earlier as well. A deep understanding of interaction teaches you to do better, pushes you in the right direction, and in the end all users win.

I talked about examples of mistakes at the mental model level in the article [3 examples where accessibility helped earn money, surface bugs, and resolve disputes](https://habr.com/ru/companies/dododev/articles/718416/)

## Social responsibility of IT

Not everything is measured in numbers and profit. Any major company bears responsibility for its influence on people: it can help, hinder, create celebrations, drown out voices. All of IT is now responsible for how we live.

By adapting apps and websites, we give blind people a way to interact with the world: they likewise order food delivery, take taxis, pay bills, communicate, read news and social media, go to work. By not adapting applications, we take away a piece of their life and functionality.

Applications and services are even more necessary for blind people than for everyone else. Imagine how hard it is to go to the store: walking with a cane to the store, choosing products (how?), going back home. There's a way a thousand times easier — order delivery through an app. If the app is adapted, it's easy to learn the ingredients, the selection, enter an address, pay, and buy everything you want.

At the same time, accessibility is not a feature — it can't be measured by a deadline, done once and never returned to. Accessibility lives in people's heads: it's a set of rules that developers and designers must keep in mind when they create a new interface.

## Accessibility is a skill

Accessibility support is a good metric of company culture. All the major foreign apps from Apple, Google, and Facebook are adapted and work very well. Almost all major Russian ones are either not adapted at all or contain critical bugs.

> Tip: Build applications for people. For everyone.
